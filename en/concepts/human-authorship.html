Value 7 · founding principle

Human Authorship — authorship cannot be delegated

The seventh value of the AIAD framework is also its most structuring principle: authorship of intent is never delegated to the agent. The agent may enrich, question, refine — it may not initiate. Every Intent Statement must be written in the first person by an identifiable human.

What the value actually says

Human Authorship draws the boundary that makes AI-agent orchestration viable over the long term: the agent executes with excellence, but the intent always belongs to the human. This boundary is not a philosophical stance — it is operationalised by a mechanical rule: when in doubt about the intent, the agent ASKS, it does not invent. A guessed intent is treated as a betrayed intent. This is value 7 translated into the daily life of every session: an agent that "fills in" a missing WHY to keep the task moving commits the gravest mistake in the framework, even if the result looks plausible.

When the agent cannot decide, it emits a JNSP verdict ("I don't know") — a valid signal, never a failure.

The Human author field — the friction that marks ownership

Non-negotiable

The Human author field of the Intent Statement is mandatory and filled in deliberately — never automatically. If an agent wrote the Intent Statement, the session is invalidated. The Execution Gate cannot be passed without an Intent Statement whose author is human and identifiable.

This tiny friction is intentional: it marks the moment of ownership. Asking the agent to "draft an Intent Statement to save time" is the canonical anti-pattern — the time "saved" is paid back in intentional debt, then in drift. The framework's success indicators require an identifiable human author on 100% of Intent Statements.

Serious agentic programming vs vibe coding

External legitimation arrived in May 2026: the Human Authorship value corresponds precisely to what Martin Fowler calls "serious agentic programming" — the developer reviews and validates the generated code — as opposed to "vibe coding" (Karpathy/Fowler), where the author delegates without validation. It also prevents cognitive debt (Fowler/Joshi, 2026): the accumulation of code whose implementation is accepted without understanding the underlying conceptual model. What was not understood once will repeat itself — exactly what the Human Learnings section of the AGENT-GUIDE documents.

The field evidence: 29,585 PRs

The principle is not only defensible in theory — it describes what the industry already does at scale. Across 29,585 pull requests involving agents, merge authority remains "almost exclusively human". The human keeps the last word on what enters the product, even when the agent produced most of the code. AIAD formalises this checkpoint rather than leaving it implicit: the Execution Gate on the way in, human validation and merge on the way out.

A direct answer to the AI Act (Article 14)

Human Authorship is a direct answer to the human-oversight requirements imposed by Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 — the EU AI Act — for high-risk systems (Article 14). Where the regulation requires that a human be able to oversee, understand and correct the system, AIAD structurally guarantees that the original intent is human, archived and verifiable. The AIAD-AI-ACT governance agent makes it a Tier 1 control point, with veto power.

The human-authorship-check skill

The principle is enforced mechanically by a dedicated skill, triggered by /sdd intent, /sdd init and /aiad intention. It checks five points before any strategic artifact is recorded:

The 5 checks of the human-authorship-check skill
#CheckFailure = signal
1The Author field is an identifiable human name (not "Claude", "Agent", "AI", "TBD")Non-human author
2The 5 fields come from the user, not from a paraphrase by the agentAgent-dictated intent
3"WHY NOW" cites a concrete trigger (event, metric, deadline)Immature intent
4"OBJECTIVE" contains at least one measurable metricVague objective
5"DRIFT CRITERION" is an observable signal, not a rephrased intentInoperative safeguard

Three verdicts are possible: PASS, FAIL (a known error with an actionable correction — e.g. author "Claude" → ask for the human name), or JNSP (the human is stuck on "WHY NOW": the intent is not ripe, the writing is postponed). A JNSP is here a success of the skill — it has detected an area that must remain human.

The Intent Workshop — a purely human space

The ritual counterpart of this value is the monthly Intent Workshop: 60 minutes, purely human, no AI. The /aiad intention command only serves to prepare it (compiling the month's Intents, alignment metrics, Human Learnings) — never to run it. Even in --fast mode, the agent only skips the explanations about the ritual, never the human facilitation itself. The workshop's question sums up the whole value: "are we still building what we wanted?"
