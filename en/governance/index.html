Level 0-1 · fail-closed

Tier 1 governance — the regulatory veto right

Tier 1 governance agents are regulatory AGENT-GUIDEs injected into development sessions based on context: a project processing personal data activates AIAD-RGPD, an AI component in a European context activates AIAD-AI-ACT. They hold an absolute veto right over any non-compliant deliverable — when a SPEC conflicts with a governance agent, the governance agent prevails.

In the SDD Mode documentation hierarchy, these agents sit just below the AIAD Constitution, above the PRD, the ARCHITECTURE and the SPECs. No lower-level artifact may contradict them, and no veto verdict may be ignored — not even by the human within the normal flow of the cycle: lifting a veto requires a documented decision outside the cycle, never a silent bypass of the agent.

Uncertainty policy — fail-closed

If the agent cannot decide whether a Tier 1 framework applies — a module whose data, AI or interface footprint is unclear, an undocumented scope, a source file only partially readable — the default verdict is UNKNOWN, treated as a VETO until the ambiguity has been resolved on the human side. The agent never concludes "not applicable" from an absence of evidence. This is the application of the JNSP uncertainty policy (JNSP: "Je Ne Sais Pas" — I don't know) to the regulatory domain.

The expected form in the output of the regulatory-veto skill is structured:

verdict: UNKNOWN
referential: <AIAD-XXX>
reason: <what is missing to decide>
question: <reformulation for the human>
Against silent self-whitewashing

This rule protects against an agent that rewrites history by saying "no problem" when it could not actually read the area in question. An absence of evidence is never evidence of compliance: doubt closes the door, it does not open it.

Priority hierarchy between frameworks

Several frameworks can apply simultaneously to the same code — a chatbot processing health data accumulates AI Act, RGPD and RGAA. In case of conflict, the priority order is fixed:

  1. AI Act Art. 5 (prohibitions) — absolute priority, full stop;
  2. RGPD + Art. 9 — a reinforced legal basis is required for sensitive data, before any implementation;
  3. AI Act high-risk — procedural obligations: documentation, human oversight, logging;
  4. RGAA — the interfaces required by the AI Act (disclosure, oversight, appeal) must be accessible;
  5. RGESN — energy optimisation, in compliance with the four levels above.

One practical consequence: a frugal but inaccessible site violates the RGAA — frugality never justifies sacrificing accessibility or the protection of individuals.

AIAD-AI-ACT — Regulation (EU) 2024/1689

Triggered whenever the code involves an AI component (ML, LLM, scoring, recommendation). The agent enforces a risk-level qualification at the start of the session: unacceptable risk (Art. 5 — prohibited, escalation to leadership), high risk (Annex III — full regime: Art. 11 documentation, Art. 10 data quality, Art. 14 human oversight, Art. 72 monitoring), limited risk (Art. 50 — mandatory disclosure of chatbots and generated content) or minimal risk.

The Human Authorship principle is the framework's direct answer to the human-oversight requirements of Article 14: a system where the human authors the intent and retains the override is, by construction, aligned with the AI Act. Maximum penalties: €35M or 7% of worldwide turnover for prohibited practices.

AIAD-RGPD — Privacy by Design

Triggered whenever the code processes personal data. The guiding principle fits in one sentence: data that is not collected cannot be breached. The agent applies Privacy by Design & by Default (Art. 25) architecturally: a documented legal basis before any collection (consent, contract, legal obligation, legitimate interest…), minimisation (Art. 5.1.c), purpose and retention limitation, and implementation of data-subject rights (Art. 15-22) — access, erasure, portability, rectification, objection, with a response within 30 days. Sensitive data (Art. 9: health, biometrics, opinions, sexual orientation…) triggers a block and a systematic escalation to the DPO or the PM before implementation.

AIAD-RGAA — digital accessibility

Triggered whenever the code produces a user interface. Reference framework: RGAA 4.1.2 (decree of 20 September 2024, 106 criteria across 13 themes), built on WCAG 2.1 levels A and AA. The French legal obligation stems from Article 47 of law 2005-102 for the public sector, and since 28 June 2025 from the European Accessibility Act for a broad private-sector scope (banking, e-commerce, transport, telephony). The agent enforces built-in accessibility — alt text on images, 4.5:1 contrast ratios, form labels, keyboard navigation, visible focus, aria-current, skip link — because fixing it afterwards costs ten times more. Supervisory authority: ARCOM; penalties up to €300,000 within the EAA scope.

AIAD-RGESN — digital eco-design

Triggered by every technical decision (performance, resources, dependencies) — it is the agent with the broadest scope. Reference framework: RGESN v2 (May 2024, ARCEP / ARCOM / ADEME / DINUM, 79 criteria, 9 themes). It embodies the AIAD value of Intentional Frugality: every line of code has a real energy cost. The Algorithms theme specifically targets computational frugality: a deterministic business rule consumes 100 to 10,000 times less than an LLM inference, the smallest sufficient model must be chosen, and every LLM call is token-capped and cached. Findings quantify the impact (kWh, gCO2eq) using an ADEME or Ecoindex methodological baseline.

AIAD-CRA — Cyber Resilience Act

Triggered for any software product intended for the EU market. Reference framework: Regulation (EU) 2024/2847, in force since 10 December 2024, fully applicable on 11 December 2027 — with one obligation brought forward to 11 September 2026: reporting actively exploited vulnerabilities to ENISA within 24 hours. The agent checks the 13 essential requirements (secure-by-design, secure default configuration, encryption, minimal attack surface, signed updates…) and the 8 vulnerability-management obligations: a CycloneDX SBOM regenerated for every release, a SECURITY.md with response SLAs, free patches, a CHANGELOG referencing CVEs. Before each release tag, it blocks if the SBOM is missing or if a vulnerability with CVSS ≥ 7 is unpatched. Penalties: up to €15M or 2.5% of worldwide turnover.

Regional governance packs

The Tier 1 baseline (AI-ACT, RGPD, RGAA, RGESN, CRA) is EU/France-centric. For other jurisdictions and sector-specific contexts, governance packs add agents under the same contract: fail-closed veto, read-only, mandatory evidence.

Claude Code subagents

Under Claude Code, each Tier 1 agent also exists as a subagent in .claude/agents/ (AIAD-AI-ACT, AIAD-RGPD, AIAD-RGAA, AIAD-RGESN), generated from the single source .aiad/gouvernance/ by npx aiad-sdd emit-rules. Their execution contract is non-negotiable:

  • read-only — tools restricted to Read, Grep, Glob; Edit, Write and Bash explicitly forbidden;
  • proactive triggering — the PROACTIVELY review description and path patterns (**/api/**, **/auth/**, **/gdpr/**…) cause Claude Code to invoke them without being asked;
  • clear-cut verdict — CONFORME (compliant), NON-CONFORME (non-compliant) or UNKNOWN (= VETO), each verdict citing file:line evidence;
  • blocking veto — advisory for the model, but enforced at the PreToolUse/Stop hook level.

All three rely on the regulatory-veto skill, which issues a PASS / WARN / VETO verdict with structured remediation — and UNKNOWN = VETO when it cannot decide. Ignoring a Tier 1 veto is one of SDD Mode's absolute prohibitions, on the same level as coding without a validated SPEC.
