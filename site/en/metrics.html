Uncompromising empiricism

Metrics — measuring value, not activity

AIAD metrics inform decisions — they do not dictate them. Generated organically by the SDD cycle itself and persisted in .aiad/metrics/, they measure outcomes, never velocity.

Philosophy: outcome, not output

In a data-driven approach, the numbers decide. In AIAD's data-informed approach, the numbers illuminate: you optimise the value, not the metric, and you read trends rather than react to fluctuations. It is the framework's fifth value — uncompromising empiricism — applied to measurement itself.

The direct consequence: velocity is not measured. Counting story points or lines of code is measuring activity. SDD Mode measures what matters: does the delivered feature solve the problem, does it reflect the intent, and is the flow that produced it healthy? A good metric is actionable, understandable, comparable and honest — hard to game without genuine improvement (Goodhart's law).

The SQS — Spec Quality Score

The SQS is the cycle's entry metric: it determines whether the Execution Gate opens. Five scorable criteria — atomicity, precision, testability, non-ambiguity, defined scope — completed by a sixth, non-scorable criterion, the Stranger Test: would a person from outside the project understand the SPEC without any verbal context?

  • SQS ≥ 4/5 → Gate open, the agent can start;
  • SQS < 4/5 → back to revision, with a criterion-by-criterion remediation plan;
  • SQS partially unscorable → score ?/5 and Gate UNKNOWN — JNSP verdict, human decision required. Never any self-clearance.
EARS bonus (v1.11)

An EARS-format SPEC whose strict lint (rules R1-R7) raises no violation earns a guaranteed +1 on the Testability criterion; a single violation forces that criterion to 0.

The SDD metrics

Specific to the cycle, they measure the health of the spec-anchored process. They are computed by /aiad retro and aggregated by the dashboards:

MetricWhat it measuresTarget
Average SQSQuality of the SPECs delivered over the period.≥ 4/5 — alert below 3.5/5
Drift rateSPECs out of sync with the code, detected by /sdd drift-check.Downward trend
First-time success rateAgent sessions that succeed on the first attempt, with no corrective iteration.> 70%
Intent/delivery alignment rateIntent Statements whose DRIFT CRITERION is honoured — checked at the Intent Workshop.> 80%

A first-time success rate below 70% points to an outdated AGENT-GUIDE or poorly written SPECs; an alignment rate below 80% questions the quality of the Intent Statements themselves.

DORA Metrics

The four indicators from the DORA programme (Google) measure delivery performance. AIAD generates them organically from .aiad/metrics/deployments/ — no extra data entry. The command: /aiad dora.

The 4 DORA metrics and the Elite-level threshold
MetricWhat it measuresElite level
Deployment FrequencyHow often code is deployed to production.≥ 1/day
Lead Time for ChangesCommit → production delay (AIAD also measures the extended Lead Time, intent → production).< 1 hour
Change Failure RatePercentage of deployments causing an incident.< 5%
MTTRMean time to recovery after an incident.< 1 hour

The report positions each metric on the Elite / High / Medium / Low scale, then traces back to verifiable AIAD causes: non-atomic SPECs, a Gate repeatedly closed, low SQS, governance skipped. Don't aim for Elite right away — one level per quarter is realistic progress, and an honest Change Failure Rate is worth more than an untraced hotfix.

Flow Metrics

Where DORA evaluates delivery, Flow Metrics evaluate the health of the value stream, from idea to deployment. The command: /aiad flow.

The 5 Flow Metrics
MetricWhat it measuresBenchmark
Cycle TimeExecution → deployment duration (85th percentile, robust to outliers).< 3 days
Lead TimeIntent created → deployment duration, with a per-step breakdown.< 2 weeks
ThroughputSPECs delivered per week.Stable or rising
WIPSPECs in progress simultaneously.Explicit limit per PE
Flow EfficiencyActive time / total time.15-40% (normal)

The most valuable analysis is the Lead Time breakdown, which locates the bottlenecks: a 15-day Lead Time with a 2-day Cycle Time reveals 13 days of hidden waiting — often between the Intent and the SPEC, or in Gate iterations. Little's law (Lead Time = WIP / Throughput) is the lever: to go faster, reduce WIP or increase Throughput.

The dashboards

The two AIAD dashboards
CommandFormatUsage
/aiad dashboardASCII, in the chatTeam ritual — weekly (operational, 15-20 min) or monthly (strategic, 30-45 min, with stakeholders).
/aiad dashboard-htmlStatic multi-page HTML in dashboard/Continuous steering — intents, SPECs, traceability, metrics, governance, drifts, changelog. Regenerated on demand, publishable (GitHub Pages).

Both draw on the same .aiad/metrics/ persistence (deployments, specs, standup, drift, retro…). Three signals on the weekly dashboard trigger immediate action: a Cycle Time that doubles, an average SQS below 3.5/5, a Change Failure Rate above 15%.

Missing data = process signal

An untraced deployment is not a hole to fill with an estimate — it is a signal that the process is leaking. The metrics commands alert explicitly rather than averaging over an incomplete sample.

The continuous improvement loop

Metrics only make sense plugged into a PDCA loop: identify a problem through the metrics, formulate a hypothesis, change at small scale, measure before/after, standardise or retry. Two commands support it:

  • /aiad retro — the retrospective collects Lessons Learned and Human Learnings, computes the iteration's metrics and commits to 1 to 3 actions at most, checked at the next retrospective;
  • /sdd context — the post-session feedback loop compares the estimated Context Engineering Budget to the actual one (tokens, degradation, duration) and recommends optimisations for the next session.

Cadence: automatic daily monitoring, weekly team review (retrospective), monthly outcomes review (strategic sync), quarterly review of the framework itself. Start with five to seven essential metrics — measuring everything means steering nothing.
